*Lingvar Thenatis Uvedruns, that is, The Chronicles of the Kings of Lingvar, Part One, the manuscript of the blackrobe Devins of Vos Daur Loika*

## 1. Of the Beginnings of Mankind
When the Ösbairgs[1] were raised upon the trunk of Madgarrian[2], the Lesars[3] sat on thrones and ruled over the peoples.

Many longed to break free of their dominion, and those who succeeded fled to Lukar[4]. There, having tamed the beasts, they founded realms of their own. These are the forefathers of the Rethens, the Hurs, the Sanvigs, and the Narevings. The rulers hunted them so far as their power reached, and those taken were brought back to the city. To hinder further flight, the walls of Ösbairgs were raised higher still, and guards were set round about the stronghold.

In those days there lived a man called Telvars, who was heeded among men. He sought a way out of bondage, yet knew not where to flee, for the might of the rulers stretched far. One day his wife dreamed of a writhing knot of serpents. And when Galauksna[5] shone above them, the serpents were struck blind. When Telvars heard the telling of the dream, he gave this reading of it:

“Where the star gives light, there the rule of the rulers does not reach. There shall we find shelter.”

Having spoken thus, he bade them watch for the star. And when they saw it in the southern sky, they resolved to leave Ösbairgs. Now it so befell that Telvars’s son-in-law, who had taken part in the raising of the walls, had in secret hollowed out a passage, unknown to the others, and called it Busvrath[6]. It was so narrow that one could scarcely force oneself through it. By that way the fugitives made their escape from the city.

Fortune was with them. Following the star, Telvars came to the sea. There he halted, for none of his people knew how to build boats. The sea was called Nasel, for it breathed forth vapours no beast and no man could endure. The runaways dwelt in caves they found among rocky cliffs. That land they named Peligrems[7], because many birds nested there.

The Lesars did not draw near to the coast. The mists of Nasel were perilous to them. And the folk of Telvars did not leave the caves, but lived on what the sea brought them. So many years passed. Telvars died, and they raised him a tomb upon a hill.

Then it came to pass that flat boats from the south put in at the shores of Peligrem, to the wonder of its dwellers. It was found that the Adals of Hilma[8], though they knew not even sails, alone possessed the art of crossing Nasel. And when they met the Telvarids, they were greatly astonished, for they had thought all men had been devoured.

The Adals differed from men and did not understand them. They called them the Lost, marvelling at their customs[9]. They themselves worshipped no gods and made no sacrifice. They dressed as they do still—plainly, knowing nothing of ornaments. Their clothing was made of cured skins, and their cloaks they wove of reeds.

Yet they showed the Telvarids how to build boats, and they taught them to sail along the coast. Some they even took with them, to show them Vintimmars[10]. Those men, when they returned, said there was no safer place in all the world.

But the Adals took no one farther than Hilma. Moreover, though the Telvarids had learned seafaring, none of them could cross the sea. It was held that the Adals had laid a spell upon the waters, forbidding passage to the lands of the south.

Other peoples began to call the dwellers of the coast the Irtais[11], because they could move only along the shore and did not dare the deeper sea.

###### 2. Of Burrik’s Voyage
Among them was a man called Tovs, who, though he himself had never sailed to Vintimmars, had learned the craft of shipbuilding. He had two sons, and to them he passed on his knowledge, so that none could match their boats.

The elder brother, Burrik[12], desired to build a ship that might cross Nasel. For both he and many among the Irtais feared that one day the Lesars would find a way to seize Peligrems. And indeed, as though to prove their fear well founded, envoys of the Khagan began to come from the inland, demanding tribute[13].

Burrik and his brother, whose name was Oltis, built a boat after the manner of the Adals. He set a sail upon it and named it Skrels[14], for it was to fly over the waters like a bird. Yet others called it Drasvarg[15], because Burrik crowned its prow with a serpent’s head. And when the Adals beheld it, they were sore afraid.

Some say that Burrik received from a sorcerer of the Narevings[16] a pouch with enchanted wind bound within it. Others held that it was by means of the sail that he crossed the mists, or else that the spell barring the way to Vintimmars had somehow been broken.

The Adals placed upon the prows of their boats lanterns made of latir[17]. These gave enough light that sailors might not lose their course upon Nasel. Burrik stole such a lamp, and after he came ashore, he burned the ship to hide his deed. Thereafter many called the man of the Irtais the first sailor, saying that none before him had reached the south, but the truth shall come to light.

When the ship was made ready, Burrik gathered a company and set out on the voyage, telling nothing of it to his people. To guard his inheritance he left the younger, Oltis, who from that time ruled the Irtais in Peligrem.

Let it suffice to say that Burrik voyaged south in the early autumn, three hundred years after Ösbairgs had been abandoned. He passed by Hilma, and after some days came to the shores of Vintimmars. Finding the mouth of the Nareva, he sailed upriver.

###### 3. The Prophecy of Laidane
He landed in a riverside clearing ringed with birches, and called it Eiragala[18]. There he raised a settlement enclosed by a palisade, to guard against wild beasts.

When the Gavalds[19] heard that men had come, their chieftain, Orvar[20], went to meet them. And thus it happened.

When he learned whence they had come, Orvar summoned the Adals who were in his settlement, to serve as interpreters. Burrik he invited to an islet in midstream. There grew a great linden, held in especial honour by the vilas. Winter and summer alike it lost no leaf, but stood untouched through all the year.

So when Burrik came with his warriors, they saw Orvar’s wife[21] beneath the linden, tending the bee-trees. It seemed to them that they looked upon a giantess or some other deity, and they bowed before her. Then she turned to them and spoke thus:

*No dark shadow rules*<br>
*the land of the sun.*<br>
*Leave it beyond the mist*<br>
*ere it slips through hearts.*<br>

*Change is carried*<br>
*by the breath of boughs.*<br>
*A wise man hears it*<br>
*when doom is spoken.*<br>

Then Orvar came forth with the translators. Burrik marvelled that the vilas were unarmed, and moreover clad in plain garments without adornment. The chieftain welcomed the Irtais, wondering that they had crossed the sea. He asked also what life was like in the lands where the Lesars held sway. Burrik answered all things truthfully, save that he hid the means by which he had reached Vintimmars. At last Orvar, moved by the flight of the Irtais and their hard fate, took off his torc and gave it to Burrik.

Burrik received it gladly, the more so because the neck-ring was made of gold. Yet he set little store by the chieftain’s words.

The people settled at the mouth of the Nareva, where the lands were fertile and the forests teemed with game. Burrik chose out a hill and built a stronghold there, and named it Kalva, while he proclaimed himself prince. Orvar’s necklace he had reforged into a crown, which from then on he and his heirs wore. It was named Ösvöldis[22].

There were plans also to send boats north, to bring settlers from there. But Burrik had no more latir from which lanterns might be made, and without them no one could cross Nasel. The vilas, though bound in friendship, would not give men any latir. They feared it might fall into the hands of the rulers.

###### 4. Of the Breaking of Friendship
Burrik promised Orvar that he would not sail to the shores of Peligrem. Yet in the secrecy of his heart he had no mind to keep that oath.

In those days the vilas did not yet hide themselves from men, but shared with them their knowledge. Stolims [giants] also dwelt in the mountains, stone-folk and agras hid in the forests, and a host of other strange beings inhabited Noricia. But strangest of all were the vilas, who differ from us not only in shape, but live unlike all other peoples.

Burrik was often a guest of Orvar, and men made free use of the gardens of the vilas [23]. And the vilas helped the newcomers as best they could, giving them food and clothing. Yet they possessed not those things that delight the eye of a ruler.

When Burrik came to know the Gavalds better, he began to despise them, and among his own he called their leader the forest king, or worse besides. He made light of both friendship and the help he had been given, for in his sight they counted for little. The mountains to the south tempted him, for he had heard they hid silver. Yet more did he desire latir.

Now among the prince of the Irtais’ company there was a man named Aislar. Yet men called him Rohumo[24], for he was cunning and knew many tricks. Some said he was the son of a witch. This was the man whom Burrik urged to steal latir for him.

Feigning himself a singer, Rohumo persuaded the Gavalds to teach him their tales. Thus he learned where they hid latyr, and how they drew it from mountain lakes and gathered it from the sea.

When Burrik learned this, he gathered warriors and promised each of them much, so long as they would obey him. And when the time came, he went to the road where the vilas carried the latir, and fell upon them. All were slain. Not even the cattle that bore the jars of latyr were spared. And since there had been no guard with them, no glory came of it.

Now, when the ruler of Kalva had latir in his hands, he brought forth the boats he had earlier ordered made in secret. Nine of them he sent north, to his brother Oltis, with these words:

“Come to me, and bring with you as many as ever you can.”

And many further inducements he added besides, thinking that soon he would rule far more than those he had brought with him. He ordered Rohumo too to go, for tidings had reached Burrik of what manner of man he was, and he wished to be rid of him. But Rohumo discovered his lord’s intent, and because in the secrecy of his heart he served other powers, he resolved to betray him.

The bodies of the slain Burrik ordered buried in the forest. What would not have passed among men, he accomplished with the vilas. When Orvar told him of the disappearance of his people, Burrik feigned astonishment. Then he lied and said that his own folk too sometimes vanished without trace.

“Perhaps,” said he, “some new evil has arisen in these lands, of which you do not yet know, lord.”

And Orvar believed his words, for the vilas knew not falsehood and were more credulous than they became in later years.

###### 5. The Death of Burrik
The boats that had been sent soon came to Peligrem. The envoys went to Oltis, to bow before him and bear him tidings from his brother. But when they came there, they found another ruled the land, and Oltis was dead.

For the Khagan had come to him and said thus:

“Pay me tribute, from cave or house alike. Else the Lesars shall hear of you, they shall lay waste your land, and take you for their own.”

Oltis would not pay, thinking he could still hide in the caves, as he had done before. But Presteving, who was his chamberlain, cast him from the throne before war had even begun.

Now he received the envoys courteously and questioned them about everything. Yet they were afraid to speak openly with him, suspecting deceit. So Presteving dismissed them, trusting that in due time he would learn the truth of their coming. In the night, when he lay down to sleep, Rohumo came to him.

“If you would master the sea,” he said to Presteving, “you must seize the lanterns they have hidden in the barrels. Vintimmars is rich in every good thing, and above all there are no Lesars there. Burrik is weak, and he may easily be swept aside.”

Having spoken thus, he told the ruler of Peligrem all that he knew. Presteving praised him and promised him reward for his betrayal.

Rohumo also persuaded the ruler to give him several men, but did not say for what purpose. In secret from all others he sent them to the Khagan, wishing to inform the rulers of what Burrik had done, and also of the manner in which men had overcome the mists of Nasel.

On the next day the boats were searched, and the barrels with the lanterns were taken. The envoys were cast into prison. Then Presteving gathered his men and set out in thirty boats. He himself sailed first with his war-band, and the settlers followed after.

When Burrik saw the boats-and they were the same ones he had sent to Peligrem-he rejoiced and went out to meet them. But Rohumo leapt into the water, calling the prince to come and greet his brother. Burrik stepped out onto the shore, and then Rohumo struck him and clove his skull. Then the boats with the warriors came in.

The men of Burrik fled to Kalva. Presteving pursued them, and when they would not yield the stronghold, he set fire to it. Since Kalva was guarded only by a palisade, and when it burned, battle was joined. There the warriors of Burrik were slain, and the stronghold was destroyed to the last.

The vilas, thinking a fire had broken out, hastened to Kalva to bring aid, and many of them perished. And when they had gone, the waters of the Nareva rose over their banks and flooded the islet where the marvellous linden had stood, so that it was seen no more. I have heard tales that this came to pass because of the tears Laidane shed in grief for the death of so many of her kin.

Presteving took Ösvöldis, and not far from the burned stronghold he built a hall. In honour of his former dwelling he named it Irstva[25]. He divided the land and gave it to his warriors. And when Rohumo came to him, he set him upon a boat and pushed him out into the sea, saying:

*Lust was your guide*<br>
*when you left your lord.*<br>
*Orvar and Burrik*<br>
*reckon your treason.*<br>

*So rule waste land*<br>
*till your days go dark.*<br>
*For all your deeds,*<br>
*this is your reward!*<br>

Rohumo was seen no more.

After all this, Presteving had himself crowned king in the place where the garden of the vilas had stood, and there he made a sacred place and offered sacrifices. Such was the beginning of rule in Norycja.

*Here ends the manuscript of Devins.*


##### Footnotes

[1] Madgarrian, nor. literally, “the Great Tree.”

[2] Ösbairgs, nor. “the Golden Hill.” Later this city was also called Ausardis, that is, “the Golden Enclosure.”

[3] Lesars: etymology unknown. In the Chronicle the term is synonymous with “the Rulers.” The name seems to refer both to the wyrms of Ausardis and to their human representatives.

[4] Lukar, or Lukaria: an obscure name. The steppes around Ausardis were also called Gal Tautuns, nor. “the White Fields.”

[5] Galauksna, nor. “the White Star.” The vilas called it Nerianeline, “the Flower-Lady of Men,” and held that Sōline herself had hung it in the sky.

[6] Busvrath, nor. “the Mouse Gate.”

[7] Peligrems, nor. “the Song of Birds.”

[8] The Adals: a tribe of vilas from the island of Adalsia. Hilma is the Norycian name of the island of Adalsia, which lies upon the Nasel Sea.

[9] Possibly a later interpolation. Such words do indeed exist in Areile — for instance glevinar, “sad, dark, lost.”

[10] Vintimmars, nor. “Beyond the Shadow.” A term for the lands south of the Nasel Sea.

[11] That is, “the Rowers”; nor.

[12] Burrik, nor. “the Sailor.”

[13] This refers to the Dragon Khaganate, a confederation of tributary tribes subject to the Lesars. It is possible that in Burrik’s time it did not yet exist. In the case of the Khaganate, tribute meant the yearly surrender of a set number of youths or maidens. From the later course of the tale it appears that the Khaganate may also have levied payments in gold.

[14] Skrels, nor. “Winged” or else “Gleaming.”

[15] Drasvargs, nor. “the Great Dragon.”

[16] Undoubtedly a later interpolation.

[17] Latir: a corrupted name. What is meant is kalatār, the grey amber drawn from the coasts of Noricia.

[18] Eiragala, or Ergolla: nor., etymology unclear. The author derives it from eiri gal, that is, “in the midst of the white.” Yet it is also possible that the name comes from Orvar’s byname, Areigal, “Wind-Speaker.”

[19] The Gavalds: a tribe of vilas dwelling in the basin of the Nareva.

[20] Chieftain — literally labban, “leader.” A more fitting word would be “prince,” or even “king.”

[21] “Orvar’s wife”: this was Laidane of the Kenalds, from the Velsire Mountains.

[22] Ösvöldis, nor. “the Golden Cord.”

[23] The gardens of the vilas were demarcated tracts of forest where fruit trees and berry-bearing shrubs were cultivated.

[24] Rohumo, nor. “grass snake.”

[25] Irstva, nor. “the Pit.” The author here refers to the famed port of Irstvabarg. In truth, the stronghold arose much later. After the destruction of Kalva, men dwelt not at the mouth of the Nareva for a long while.
