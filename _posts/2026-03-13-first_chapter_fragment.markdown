## Chapter one
You could smell rain in the air. Heavy rain, as though before the end of the world.

A man stood in the shadow of a stone pillar. A long scarf of an indistinct color was wrapped around his neck, falling over the lapels of a worn jacket. Though he was tall, he stooped. His hair was cut short, and his features were delicate, almost feminine, which he tried to conceal with a beard. He was young, thin, and his clothes marked him as low-born.

He watched a carriage making its way up the cobbled street. The first rays of sunlight were appearing over the hills that encircled the city. The man glanced at his watch. It was nearly seven. The date window still showed the half-emerged figure of a one. The watch was old, its cover bent back. On the dial, in italics, were the words “G. Pekaris.” The dawn spreading across the sky was blurred by the fog hanging over the city.

The man stepped toward the carriage as it came to a halt beneath the bell tower. He stopped just in front of the cab without peering inside. A figure emerged from the carriage, dressed in a thick brown frock coat. On his head he wore a shabby hat with a narrow brim.

“You the new one?”

“They call me Fox.”

The figure in the coat turned out to be a broad-shouldered fellow with huge hands. He was shorter than Fox, though much more solidly built. A little older too, with a stern, square face. His mustache was dark and curled upward at the ends. In his hands he carried a leather bag, like the kind doctors used, only larger.

“The shovel’s in the back,” he said, heading toward the gate. “My name’s Brastan.”

“To the cemetery?”

A few stalls stood by the entrance, the kind that sold flowers during feast days. Brastan passed them and pushed open a creaking iron gate. He strode on without looking back at his companion.

The old city cemetery occupied the southeastern slope of Cemetery Hill, stretching far downward, almost to the Nalewka stream flowing toward the walls. There were no trees here. As far as the eye could see, meadows spread all around, still pale and not yet green after winter. Beyond the massive brick wall that enclosed the cemetery began a sparse conifer forest.

Fox did not come here often. His trade kept him in the inhabited parts of the city.

Brastan, with the other man following two paces behind, made for the oldest part of the cemetery. They walked among overgrown gravestones, many of them decayed, surrounded by yellowed grass. Only a small number of graves looked as if anyone still tended them. The rest had fallen into ruin. The new cemetery, the one in use for the past several years, lay on the other side of the river.

Behind them, mausoleums crowded together like a miniature village. Most were overgrown with dark ivy, which, the farther they moved from the gate, seemed more and more to devour everything that rose above the level of the weeds.

Some of the mausoleums were little more than heaps of rubble. From others, stunted trees had grown out of the interior. Worst of all were the ones whose tombs had collapsed inward, gaping now with dark, unsettling hollows. The former inhabitants of Bersavir had not cared much for the law requiring cremation.

“What are we looking for?” the younger man asked.

With a flick of his bag, Brastan indicated the nearest gravestone.

“A corpse.”

Rummaging in the pocket of his coat, he produced a small vial and emptied its contents into his palm. Fox caught sight of tiny crystals the color of dirty violet. Crushing them between his fingers, Brastan sprinkled the powder over the nearest grave.

Something should have happened, but nothing did. The man ground out a curse under his breath.

“Damn alchemists and their concoctions. I said it wouldn’t work.”

“What is that, anyway?”

The other man muttered something and moved on to the next burial plot, repeating the action.

“Powder, New Boy. Can’t you see?”

Only on the sixth or seventh try did anything change. The violet dust scattered across the stone slab began to turn a pale yellowish color, strongest around a crack where the slab met the pedestal.

Brastan spat at the sight.

“Hand me the shovel,” he said.

The two of them managed to pry the stone aside and shift it away, revealing the opening of the tomb. The older man struck a match and tossed it down into the dark pit. Fox glanced around nervously. The cemetery was empty, unless one counted a few cats warming themselves in the paths. Nor had anyone ever heard of the guards venturing onto the Hill. And yet they were out in the open.

I didn’t sign up for this kind of work, Fox thought.

“What exactly are we doing here?” he asked. “You’re not grave robbers. At least, Vurders never said anything like that.”

Brastan merely grimaced, then scratched his clean-shaven jaw. He reached into the bag and took out a flashlight. Despite the expression of apparent boredom on his face, his gray eyes were studying Fox.

“You’re asking me? It’s his plan.”

“Whose?”

“You know perfectly well,” he said at last. “Vurders surely didn’t neglect to explain that. The real question is—who are you?”
